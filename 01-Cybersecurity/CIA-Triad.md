## Overview

The CIA Triad is one of the fundamental models of information security. It defines the three core principles used to protect information systems: **Confidentiality**, **Integrity**, and **Availability**. Nearly every security control, policy, and technology aims to preserve one or more of these principles.

## Confidentiality

Confidentiality ensures that information is only accessible to authorized users. It prevents unauthorized disclosure of sensitive data through mechanisms such as authentication, access control, encryption, and the principle of least privilege.

## Integrity

Integrity ensures that data remains accurate, complete, and trustworthy throughout its lifecycle. It protects information from unauthorized modification or destruction by using mechanisms such as hashing, digital signatures, version control, and access permissions.

## Availability

Availability ensures that systems, services, and data remain accessible whenever authorized users require them. It is maintained through redundancy, backups, fault tolerance, monitoring, disaster recovery, and protection against attacks such as Denial of Service (DoS).

## Balancing the Triad

The three principles often complement each other but may also require trade-offs. For example, increasing confidentiality through stricter access controls may reduce availability, while improving availability through redundancy must not compromise confidentiality or integrity. Effective security seeks an appropriate balance between all three principles based on the organization's needs.
